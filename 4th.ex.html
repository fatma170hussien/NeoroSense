Your Wellness Path

Track your progress and collect badges for your efforts!

Depression

Brighten your day with clear, uplifting activities.

Gratitude Journal

Write 3 things you're thankful for to focus on positivity (Emmons & McCullough, 2003).

Sunlight Walk

Walk outside for 20 minutes to boost serotonin with natural light.

Joy Jar

Collect notes of happy moments in a jar for tough days.

Music Playlist

Curate a list of uplifting songs to elevate your mood.

Task Triumph

Complete one small task (e.g., tidy a drawer) and celebrate it (Cuijpers et al., 2007).

"Small wins light up the darkest days."

PTSD

Find calm and safety with grounding activities.

Sensory Grounding

Use the 5-4-3-2-1 method to stay present (Bisson et al., 2015).

Safe Space Visualization

Imagine a comforting, safe place in detail.

Memory Journal

Write about a positive, safe moment to anchor emotions.

Breathing Anchor

Practice slow, deep breaths for 8 minutes to calm arousal.

Texture Touch

Explore calming textures (e.g., soft fabric) to stay grounded.

"You are the anchor in your storm."

Anxiety Disorder

Soothe worries with clear, calming steps.

Worry Jar

Write worries and store them for later review (Borkovec et al., 1983).

Body Scan

Focus on each body part to release tension for 12 minutes.

Emotion Sketch

Draw your feelings as shapes or colors to externalize them.

Grounding Walk

Walk slowly, noticing each step, for 10 minutes.

Thought Challenge

Write an anxious thought and counter it with evidence.

"Worries fade when you take control."

Schizophrenia

Build stability with structured, clear activities.

Daily Schedule

Create a visual daily plan to reduce stress (Eack et al., 2013).

Calming Music

Listen to soothing instrumental music for 12 minutes.

Story Writing

Write a short story about a positive memory.

Art Expression

Draw or color to express emotions safely.

Routine Check

Review and adjust your daily routine for consistency.

"Structure is your steady foundation."

Social Anxiety

Build confidence with simple, empowering steps.

Social Steps

Start with small interactions, like smiling at someone (Hofmann & Otto, 2017).

Role-Play Practice

Rehearse a social scenario in front of a mirror.

Self-Affirmations

Write 3 positive qualities about yourself.

Conversation Prompts

Create a list of easy conversation starters.

Comfort Object

Carry a small, comforting item during social events.

"Your courage grows with every interaction."

ADHD

Channel energy with clear, focused activities.

Puzzle Challenge

Solve a simple puzzle to boost focus (Ratey & Hagerman, 2008).

Energy Break

Do 5 minutes of high-energy movement like dancing.

Task Timer

Work on a task for 5 minutes with a timer.

Reward System

Create a reward for completing a small task.

Fidget Focus

Use a fidget tool to stay engaged during tasks.

"Your energy is your unique strength."

Autism Spectrum

Support your needs with clear, calming tasks.

Sensory Kit

Assemble calming items like a stress ball (Schaaf & Lane, 2015).

Visual Schedule

Create a colorful daily plan for predictability.

Interest Story

Write or draw about a favorite topic.

Calming Patterns

Draw repetitive patterns to soothe.

Social Prep

Write a short script for a social situation.

"Your unique perspective is a gift."

Bipolar Disorder

Balance your mood with consistent, clear habits.

Mood Tracker

Log daily moods to spot patterns (Harvey et al., 2015).

Nature Walk

Spend 20 minutes in nature to promote calm.

Mood Journal

Write about your day to process emotions.

Sleep Routine

Create a consistent sleep schedule.

Balance Board

List one calming and one energizing activity for balance.

"Balance is built one choice at a time."

OCD

Manage thoughts with clear, practical steps.

Thought Delay

Pause intrusive thoughts by counting to 10 (Foa et al., 2012).

Craft Focus

Engage in a simple craft like origami to shift focus.

Gratitude List

Write 5 things you're thankful for to redirect thoughts.

Pattern Break

Change one routine to disrupt compulsive loops.

Focus Object

Use a small object to anchor attention during urges.

"You are stronger than your thoughts."
