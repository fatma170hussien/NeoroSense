What is Schizophrenia?

Schizophrenia

Schizophrenia is a chronic mental health disorder that affects thinking, perception, emotions, and behavior. It goes beyond normal unusual experiences, causing persistent symptoms that significantly impair daily life, work, school, and relationships. Symptoms often begin in late adolescence or early adulthood.

Difference Between Normal Unusual Experiences and Schizophrenia

Criterion Normal Unusual Experiences Schizophrenia
Trigger Occasional and linked to stress or fatigue Symptoms occur persistently without clear triggers and are disproportionate
Duration Temporary, resolve naturally Persistent months to years, requires treatment
Severity & Impact Mild, little impact on daily life Severe impairment in social, occupational, or academic areas
Physical & Cognitive Signs Minor disturbances Hallucinations, delusions, disorganized thinking, poor attention, memory problems
Response to Support Improve naturally or with reassurance Requires long-term treatment, often medication plus therapy
Nature of Thoughts Unusual thoughts rarely persistent Persistent false beliefs, hallucinations, disorganized speech, social withdrawal

Symptoms of Schizophrenia

Positive Symptoms

(Excess of normal functions)

  • Hallucinations (hearing or seeing things not present)
  • Delusions (false beliefs, e.g., persecution, grandeur)
  • Disorganized speech or behavior
  • Agitated body movements

Negative Symptoms

(Loss of normal functions)

  • Flattened affect (reduced emotional expression)
  • Social withdrawal or isolation
  • Lack of motivation, interest, or pleasure
  • Difficulty starting and sustaining activities

Cognitive Symptoms

  • Difficulty concentrating or making decisions
  • Poor memory or planning skills
  • Trouble focusing or paying attention
  • Problems with executive functioning

Causes & Risk Factors

Biological Factors

  • Genetics (family history increases risk)
  • Brain chemistry and structure differences
  • Dopamine imbalance, enlarged ventricles
  • Neurodevelopmental factors

Psychological Factors

  • Early life stress or trauma
  • Maladaptive coping strategies
  • Personality traits and vulnerabilities
  • Poor stress management skills

Environmental Factors

  • Prenatal exposure to infection or malnutrition
  • Childhood adversity or social stress
  • Substance abuse (especially cannabis)
  • Urban upbringing or social isolation

Treatment Options

Medications

Antipsychotics (risperidone, olanzapine, aripiprazole), Long-acting injectable antipsychotics

Psychotherapy

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for psychosis (CBTp), Social skills training, Family education and support

Psychosocial Support

Supported employment, Housing assistance, Peer support, Rehabilitation programs

Lifestyle Management

Regular routines, Stress management, Healthy diet, Exercise, Sleep hygiene

Self-Help & When to Seek Help

Self-Help & Coping

  • Maintaining daily routine
  • Avoiding alcohol and drugs
  • Tracking symptoms and triggers
  • Seeking social support
  • Practicing relaxation techniques
  • Setting realistic goals
  • Learning about the condition

When to Seek Help

  • If persistent hallucinations, delusions, or disorganized behavior occur
  • If daily functioning at work, school, or home is impaired
  • If self-harm or aggression occurs
  • Early intervention is crucial for better outcomes
  • If experiencing social withdrawal or emotional flatness
  • If cognitive difficulties interfere with daily life
